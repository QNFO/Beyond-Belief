## CHAPTER 4: THE PERCEPTUAL FUNCTION — PROFOUND SALIENCE

### 4.1: Defining "Profound Salience" as a Psychological Category

1. The concept of profound salience represents the foundational perceptual function through which human meaning-making systems operate—the capacity to perceive certain aspects of reality as carrying ultimate significance, weight, or importance that distinguishes them from ordinary experience. In cognitive psychology, salience refers to the quality by which certain stimuli stand out relative to their neighbors, capturing attention through contrast, novelty, intensity, or personal relevance. Profound salience represents an extreme form of this cognitive phenomenon, where the standing out feels not merely attention-worthy but reality-revealing, not just interesting but existentially significant. This perceptual capacity enables humans to experience certain moments, objects, ideas, or relationships as portals to deeper understanding, as manifestations of ultimate reality, or as touchpoints with what feels most real and important. Across diverse traditions, this function operates whether the object of perception is conceived as God, Brahman, Sunyata, Nature, Beauty, Truth, or Moral Law—what varies is the content deemed profoundly salient, not the underlying perceptual capacity to experience something as such.

2. Distinguishing ordinary from profound salience requires examining both quantitative and qualitative differences in how experiences register in consciousness. Ordinary salience operates on a gradient: a flashing light captures attention more than steady illumination, a sudden noise interrupts background sounds, a familiar face stands out in a crowd. These are differences of degree within ordinary consciousness. Profound salience represents a difference in kind: the experience feels qualitatively distinct from ordinary perception, marked by what philosopher William James called "noetic quality"—the sense that what is perceived carries authoritative insight or reveals fundamental truth. While ordinary salience says "notice this," profound salience says "this matters ultimately" or "this reveals reality's true nature." This qualitative distinction explains why profound salience experiences often become pivotal in people's lives—conversion experiences, mystical insights, aesthetic epiphanies, scientific breakthroughs—moments when perception feels revelatory rather than merely informative.

3. Operationalizing profound salience for cross-cultural study requires identifying consistent phenomenological features reported across diverse contexts while respecting cultural variations in interpretation. Research suggests several recurring characteristics: intensity of attention that feels effortless or involuntary, emotional tone ranging from awe to peace to urgency, sense of expanded or clarified reality, feeling of connection to something larger than oneself, and lasting impact on values or life direction. The Perceptual Salience Scale developed by researchers attempts to measure these dimensions: depth (how fundamentally significant the experience feels), breadth (how comprehensively it applies), durability (how lasting its impact), and centrality (how core it becomes to identity). These measurable dimensions allow comparison across traditions while acknowledging that different cultures may cultivate sensitivity to different types of salience—some emphasizing numinous presence, others emphasizing emptiness, others emphasizing moral clarity, others emphasizing natural beauty. The common thread is the human capacity to perceive some aspects of reality as carrying weight beyond their immediate practical or sensory properties.

4. Historical precursors to the concept of profound salience appear in philosophical and religious writings across traditions, often described through metaphor because direct description proves challenging. Plato's theory of forms posited that certain experiences provide glimpses of eternal realities behind sensory appearances. Buddhist teachings describe "mindfulness" (sati) and "clear comprehension" (sampajañña) as cultivated capacities to perceive things as they really are (yathābhūta). Jewish and Christian mystics wrote of "contemplation" that perceives divine presence in all things. Romantic poets described "spots of time" when ordinary perception yields to visionary intensity. Phenomenological philosophers described "bracketing" ordinary assumptions to perceive phenomena in their suchness. While these traditions use different terminology and make different metaphysical claims, they all point toward a perceptual capacity that can be trained to register deeper dimensions of reality than ordinary consciousness typically accesses. This suggests profound salience represents not occasional anomaly but latent human capacity that different traditions cultivate through specific practices and conceptual frameworks.

5. Concrete examples of profoundly salient experiences across traditions demonstrate both the diversity of content and similarity of structure. A Buddhist meditator perceives the impermanent, interdependent nature of all phenomena as ultimately real—what appears solid and separate reveals itself as transient and interconnected. A scientist experiences awe at mathematical equations describing physical laws that feel elegantly true beyond their practical applications. An artist perceives aesthetic qualities in ordinary objects that reveal deeper patterns or meanings. A mystic experiences union with divine reality that feels more fundamental than ordinary self-world separation. A parent perceives their child's existence as carrying ultimate significance that reorganizes all other values. A social justice activist perceives systemic injustice with moral clarity that demands response. In each case, something registers not just as fact but as truth, not just as value but as ultimate concern, not just as experience but as revelation. The objects differ dramatically, but the perceptual mode—profound salience—shares structural similarities across these diverse contexts.

6. The relationship between salience perception and meaning-making operates through what cognitive scientists call "attention economies"—the allocation of limited attentional resources shapes what we notice, value, and ultimately find meaningful. Ordinary consciousness operates within what philosopher Alfred Schutz called "the natural attitude"—taking for granted the reality and significance of everyday concerns. Profound salience experiences disrupt this natural attitude by forcing attention toward what feels ultimately real, often reorganizing attention priorities long-term. This reorganization creates meaning by establishing hierarchies of significance: what matters most versus what matters less, what deserves attention versus what can be ignored, what connects to ultimate concerns versus what remains trivial. Different traditions cultivate different attention economies through practices that train perception toward their particular understanding of ultimate reality. A mindfulness practitioner learns to notice impermanence in each moment. A contemplative Christian learns to perceive divine presence in all things. A naturalist learns to appreciate evolutionary processes in everyday biology. Each tradition's meaning system includes perceptual education—teaching what to notice as ultimately significant.

7. Understanding why salience matters for belief systems requires recognizing that perception precedes conceptualization in human experience. We don't first develop abstract beliefs and then perceive accordingly; rather, certain perceptual experiences feel compelling and then we develop beliefs to explain and extend them. Mystical experiences often precede theological understanding. Aesthetic epiphanies often precede artistic theories. Moral intuitions often precede ethical systems. Scientific observations often precede theoretical frameworks. In each case, something registers as profoundly salient—as demanding attention and explanation—and belief systems develop to make sense of these compelling perceptions. This perceptual primacy explains why attempts to change beliefs through argument alone often fail: if underlying perceptions remain unchanged, new beliefs feel abstract rather than real. Effective meaning transformation typically requires perceptual retraining—practices that help people experience reality differently, not just think about it differently. This explains the centrality of meditation, prayer, ritual, and other perceptual practices across traditions: they work at the level where meaning first emerges—in how we perceive what matters.

8. Measuring and studying profound salience presents methodological challenges that contemporary research is beginning to address through interdisciplinary approaches. Neurophenomenology combines first-person experiential reports with third-person brain imaging to identify neural correlates of salient experiences. Psychometric scales attempt to quantify dimensions like mystical experience, awe, absorption, and meaning perception. Experimental paradigms induce salience through various means: sensory deprivation, psychedelics, extreme environments, or cognitive tasks that disrupt ordinary perception. Cross-cultural studies compare reports of salient experiences across different religious and secular contexts. These approaches face inherent limitations: reducing subjective experience to measurable variables risks missing qualitative essence; comparing across frameworks risks imposing external categories; laboratory conditions differ from natural contexts. Nonetheless, converging evidence suggests profound salience represents genuine psychological category with consistent features across individuals and cultures, not merely vague metaphor or cultural construction. This empirical grounding strengthens the functional analysis by showing that the perceptual function operates through identifiable psychological mechanisms.

9. Cultural variations in what triggers profound salience reveal how universal capacity gets shaped by particular frameworks while maintaining underlying structural similarities. In theistic traditions, salience often attaches to experiences interpreted as divine presence, revelation, or grace. In non-theistic contemplative traditions, salience often attaches to experiences of impermanence, emptiness, or suchness. In naturalistic traditions, salience attaches to experiences of natural beauty, evolutionary complexity, or cosmic scale. In moral traditions, salience attaches to experiences of justice, compassion, or human dignity. These variations reflect different cosmological frameworks that tell practitioners what to look for and how to interpret what they find. Yet beneath these variations, similar perceptual capacities are being trained: focused attention, suspension of ordinary assumptions, openness to what feels fundamentally significant. This combination of universal capacity and cultural variation explains both why diverse traditions share family resemblances and why they produce such different experiences and interpretations.

10. As we proceed through this chapter, we will examine the perceptual function in detail, exploring its neurological correlates, its development across attention spectra, its cultivation through contemplative traditions, its expression in secular contexts like scientific awe, its relationship to aesthetics and morality, its extreme forms in mystical states, its integration into daily life through ritualized attention, its cultural variations, and its potential pathologies. By beginning with this definitional foundation, we establish profound salience as the gateway function through which meaning systems operate—the perceptual capacity that allows humans to experience something as ultimately real, important, or significant. This function provides raw material for the other functions: what we perceive as profoundly salient shapes our ethics (how we should act in light of what matters most), our narratives (how we explain why these things matter), our transformative practices (how we cultivate sensitivity to what matters), and our communities (how we share and reinforce what matters). Understanding this perceptual foundation thus provides key to understanding how meaning systems work from the ground up—not as abstract beliefs imposed on experience but as frameworks that emerge from and shape our most compelling perceptions of what is ultimately real and significant.

### 4.2: Neurological Correlates of Salience Detection

1. Contemporary neuroscience has begun mapping the brain networks involved in salience detection and processing, revealing that our capacity to perceive profound significance has identifiable biological underpinnings while remaining irreducible to mere brain activity. The salience network—a collection of brain regions including the anterior insula and anterior cingulate cortex—functions as a dynamic switching mechanism that identifies biologically and cognitively relevant events among myriad stimuli, prioritizing them for attention and action. This network operates continuously below conscious awareness, scanning internal and external environments for signals that demand processing because they are novel, intense, emotionally significant, or personally relevant. When something registers as profoundly salient—whether a mystical experience, aesthetic epiphany, moral insight, or scientific discovery—this network shows characteristic patterns of activation and connectivity that differ from ordinary perception. Understanding these neural correlates does not explain away profound experiences but rather reveals the biological infrastructure through which human consciousness accesses dimensions of reality that feel ultimately significant.

2. The default mode network (DMN), comprising medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, and angular gyrus among other regions, plays a particularly important role in profound salience experiences through its relationship to self-referential thinking and meaning-making. During ordinary waking consciousness, the DMN remains active when we're not focused on external tasks—when we're daydreaming, remembering the past, imagining the future, or thinking about ourselves and others. During many profound salience experiences, particularly those described as self-transcendent or unitive, DMN activity typically decreases—what neuroscientists call "hypofrontality" or reduced self-referential processing. This neural pattern correlates phenomenologically with experiences of ego dissolution, loss of self-other boundaries, and feelings of unity with something larger. The relationship appears reciprocal: reduced self-focus may facilitate profound perception, while profound perception may reduce self-focus. This neurological finding helps explain why many contemplative practices aim to quiet self-referential thinking—they're attempting to create neural conditions conducive to perceiving reality without the filter of personal narrative and concern.

3. Neurochemical factors contribute significantly to salience perception by modulating how intensely experiences register and what emotional tones they carry. Dopamine systems, particularly mesolimbic pathways, enhance salience detection by marking certain stimuli as rewarding or important, creating the "this matters" feeling. Serotonin systems influence mood and perception, with certain patterns facilitating states of openness and connection. Endogenous opioids contribute to feelings of peace and bliss that often accompany profound experiences. Stress hormones like cortisol typically decrease during meditative states, reducing anxiety and defensive perception. The specific neurochemical profile varies with the type of salience experience: mystical states may involve particular serotonin patterns, aesthetic experiences particular dopamine responses, moral insights particular oxytocin patterns related to social connection. These neurochemical dimensions help explain why profound experiences feel compelling—they engage reward and emotional systems that reinforce attention and memory for what feels significant. They also suggest why different practices (fasting, chanting, dancing, meditation) might facilitate different types of salience by creating different neurochemical conditions.

4. Research on meditation practitioners provides particularly rich data about how contemplative practice shapes the brain's salience processing over time. Long-term meditators show structural differences in brain regions associated with attention, interoception, and emotional regulation: increased cortical thickness in prefrontal regions involved in executive control, increased volume in insula involved in bodily awareness, and strengthened connectivity between prefrontal regions and limbic areas involved in emotion. Functionally, experienced meditators show more efficient salience detection—quicker recognition of what matters with less distraction by what doesn't—and greater capacity to sustain attention on chosen objects. These neural changes correspond to phenomenological reports: practitioners describe increased capacity to notice subtle experiences, decreased reactivity to trivial stimuli, and heightened sensitivity to what feels genuinely significant. This research demonstrates neuroplasticity—the brain's ability to reshape itself through practice—applied to salience perception. It suggests that profound salience represents not just occasional gift but trainable capacity, with different traditions cultivating this capacity through different practices that literally reshape neural functioning.

5. Studies of prayer and ritual reveal additional dimensions of how religious practices engage salience networks, particularly through social and emotional pathways. Prayer often activates brain regions involved in social cognition (mentalizing about others' thoughts and feelings) as practitioners relate to divine beings perceived as conscious agents. Ritual participation engages motor and sensory networks through repetitive actions while synchronizing participants' neural activity, creating shared salience through collective experience. Sacred text study engages language processing regions while also activating meaning-making networks when passages feel personally significant. These diverse practices all cultivate salience but through different neural routes: prayer through social-relational pathways, ritual through embodied synchronized pathways, study through conceptual reflective pathways. This neural diversity helps explain why traditions typically incorporate multiple practices—they're training salience perception through multiple complementary neural systems, creating more robust and multidimensional sensitivity to what the tradition considers ultimately significant.

6. The "overlapping infrastructure" hypothesis suggests that diverse profound experiences—religious, aesthetic, scientific, moral—share common neural underpinnings despite their different contents and interpretations. Neuroimaging studies comparing different types of profound experiences find overlapping activations in salience networks, reward centers, and meaning-making regions regardless of whether subjects report spiritual awe, mathematical insight, artistic appreciation, or moral elevation. This overlap suggests that the brain has generalized systems for processing "this matters fundamentally" experiences, with cultural and personal factors shaping what content gets processed through these systems. The hypothesis doesn't reduce profound experiences to mere brain activity but rather identifies common neural pathways through which diverse contents become profoundly salient. This helps explain functional similarities across traditions: despite different beliefs about what matters most, all are engaging similar perceptual capacities and neural systems, just directing them toward different objects and interpretations. The overlap also explains why people sometimes have similar phenomenological reports across different traditions—similar neural patterns produce similar experiential qualities even when interpretations differ.

7. Temporal dynamics of salience processing reveal how profound experiences unfold neurologically over milliseconds to minutes, providing insight into why they feel different from ordinary perception. Electroencephalography (EEG) studies show characteristic patterns: reduced beta waves (associated with analytical thinking) and increased theta and alpha waves (associated with relaxed attention) during meditative states. Event-related potential studies show enhanced P300 responses (associated with attention to significant stimuli) during meaningful experiences. Functional MRI studies reveal dynamic connectivity changes where salience networks briefly take control from executive networks, creating moments when what feels significant overrides ordinary goal-directed processing. These temporal patterns help explain why profound experiences often feel like interruptions or breakthroughs—neurally, they involve temporary reorganization of which networks dominate processing, allowing normally background processes (like meaning detection) to come to foreground. They also suggest why such experiences can be fleeting—the brain's executive networks typically reassert control, returning to ordinary processing modes unless specifically trained otherwise through contemplative practice.

8. Individual differences in salience processing neurology help explain why some people have frequent profound experiences while others rarely do, and why people differ in what types of experiences they find compelling. Genetic variations affect neurotransmitter systems that modulate salience detection: certain dopamine receptor polymorphisms correlate with tendency to experience awe, certain serotonin transporter variants correlate with mystical experience susceptibility, certain opioid receptor variations correlate with meditative depth. Early life experiences shape neural development in ways that affect later salience processing: secure attachment correlates with capacity for positive salience, trauma can create hypersensitivity to threat-related salience. Personality traits correlate with neural patterns: openness to experience with enhanced salience network connectivity, neuroticism with threat bias in salience detection. These individual differences don't determine destiny but create predispositions that interact with cultural and personal factors. They help explain the diversity of human experience with profound salience while affirming its natural basis in variable but measurable brain characteristics.

9. Integrating first-person experience with third-person neural data represents both challenge and opportunity for understanding profound salience. The "explanatory gap" between neural correlates and subjective experience remains philosophically problematic: knowing which brain regions activate during mystical experience doesn't tell us whether God exists or whether the experience reveals ultimate reality. However, neurophenomenological approaches that carefully correlate detailed first-person reports with neural measures can identify patterns that enhance understanding of how different types of salience differ phenomenologically and neurologically. For example, experiences described as "unitive" show different patterns than those described as "numinous," which differ from those described as "noetic." These correlations help refine our understanding of salience varieties while respecting that neural data describe biological processes, not metaphysical truths. They also provide bridges between scientific and religious discourses: scientists can study the neural processes without making metaphysical claims, while religious practitioners can acknowledge natural processes without reducing experiences to mere brain activity. This respectful differentiation of explanatory levels allows for richer understanding than either approach alone.

10. Implications of neurological research for understanding meaning systems are significant but must be carefully framed to avoid reductionism. The findings demonstrate that profound salience has biological foundations, suggesting it represents natural human capacity rather than supernatural intervention. They show that this capacity can be trained through practices that literally reshape the brain, explaining why traditions include such practices. They reveal common neural infrastructure across diverse profound experiences, helping explain functional similarities across different meaning systems. They identify individual differences that help explain diversity of religious and spiritual experience. However, these neurological insights don't invalidate metaphysical claims—they describe processes through which humans access whatever realities exist, not whether those realities exist independently. A complete understanding requires both "how" questions addressed by neuroscience and "what" questions addressed by philosophy and theology. By appreciating this complementarity, we can understand profound salience as both natural biological capacity and potential gateway to transcendent realities—not either/or but both/and, with different explanatory levels addressing different aspects of this complex human phenomenon that sits at the heart of meaning-making across cultures.

### 4.3: The Spectrum from Ordinary to Extraordinary Attention

1. Human attention operates along a continuum from scattered distraction to focused concentration to effortless absorption, with profound salience typically occurring at the focused and absorbed ends of this spectrum. Ordinary waking consciousness for most people in modern societies involves what psychologist William James called "a stream of consciousness" characterized by frequent shifts between external stimuli and internal thoughts, with attention rarely sustained on any single object for extended periods. Research suggests the average person's mind wanders approximately 47% of the time during waking hours, with attention pulled by external distractions (notifications, interruptions, environmental changes) and internal distractions (worries, plans, memories, fantasies). This scattered attention represents one end of the spectrum—what Buddhist traditions call "monkey mind" constantly swinging between objects. At the other end lie states of sustained, concentrated attention that can become so absorbed in its object that self-awareness diminishes and the subject-object distinction softens. Profound salience experiences typically occur when attention reaches these concentrated or absorbed states, suggesting that attention training represents a crucial pathway for cultivating sensitivity to what different traditions consider ultimately significant.

2. Attention as a trainable skill represents a foundational insight shared across contemplative traditions, though expressed through different metaphors and methods. Buddhist psychology distinguishes between concentration (samadhi) as focused one-pointed attention and mindfulness (sati) as open receptive awareness, with both being developed through systematic practice. Christian contemplative traditions distinguish between acquired contemplation (developed through effort) and infused contemplation (received as gift), with attention training preparing for the latter. Cognitive psychology distinguishes between focused attention (concentrating on specific stimuli) and open monitoring (observing whatever arises), both trainable through exercises. Neuroscience confirms this trainability: attention networks show plastic changes with practice, becoming more efficient at selecting relevant stimuli and ignoring distractions. This trainability has crucial implications for meaning systems: if attention can be educated, then sensitivity to what a tradition considers ultimately significant can be cultivated rather than waiting for spontaneous experiences. This explains why virtually all meaning systems include attention-training practices—they're developing the perceptual capacity needed to experience their version of ultimate reality as real and compelling rather than merely conceptual.

3. Mindfulness practices as taught in contemporary secular contexts represent one approach to attention training that illustrates basic principles while stripping away traditional metaphysical frameworks. Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) and similar programs teach participants to pay attention to present-moment experience with openness, curiosity, and non-judgment. Practices include focused attention on breath, body scans noticing physical sensations, open monitoring of thoughts and emotions, and mindful engagement with daily activities. Research shows these practices increase attentional control, reduce mind-wandering, decrease reactivity to negative stimuli, and increase appreciation of ordinary moments. While secular mindfulness typically avoids metaphysical claims about ultimate reality, it nonetheless cultivates basic attentional capacities that could theoretically be directed toward any tradition's understanding of profound salience. This demonstrates that attention training has generic benefits regardless of metaphysical framework, while also showing how traditional practices can be adapted for contemporary secular contexts. It suggests that the perceptual function has both universal aspects (attention trainability) and particular aspects (what attention gets trained to notice as ultimately significant).

4. Concentration practices across traditions reveal both similarities and differences in how attention gets trained toward profound salience. In Buddhist traditions, concentration meditation often involves focusing on breath, visual objects, mantras, or bodily sensations until attention becomes stable and absorbed (reaching jhana states). In Hindu traditions, concentration (dharana) represents the sixth limb of yoga, focusing mind on single point as preparation for meditation (dhyana) and absorption (samadhi). In Christian traditions, concentration might involve repetitive prayer (like the Jesus Prayer or rosary), visual focus on icons, or contemplative reading of scripture (lectio divina). In secular contexts, concentration might be developed through focused work, artistic practice, or athletic training. Despite different objects and frameworks, these practices share common features: selecting an attentional anchor, gently returning when mind wanders, gradually increasing stability, and eventually reaching states where attention feels effortless and deeply engaged. This cross-cultural similarity suggests that concentration represents a universal human capacity that different traditions harness to cultivate sensitivity to their particular understanding of ultimate reality.

5. Flow states described by psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi represent secular examples of absorbed attention that share features with contemplative states while occurring in activities like sports, arts, work, or hobbies. Flow involves complete immersion in an activity where attention is fully engaged, self-consciousness diminishes, time perception alters, and action feels effortless and intrinsically rewarding. While flow typically occurs during goal-directed activities rather than contemplative practice, it shares neurological similarities with meditative absorption: decreased DMN activity, altered time perception, and deep engagement with present moment. This suggests that absorbed attention represents a general human capacity that can be cultivated through various means and directed toward various objects. The difference between flow and contemplative absorption often lies in intention and interpretation: flow typically aims at mastery of skill or task completion, while contemplative absorption aims at perception of ultimate reality or self-transformation. But the underlying attentional capacity appears similar, suggesting that profound salience might be accessible through multiple pathways, not just traditional contemplative practices.

6. Mystical states as described across religious traditions often represent the extreme end of the attention spectrum where ordinary perception gives way to experiences of unity, transcendence, or revelation. While mystical experiences sometimes occur spontaneously, they often follow extended periods of attentional training through prayer, meditation, asceticism, or ritual. Descriptions share common features: intense concentration that becomes all-absorbing, dissolution of ordinary subject-object distinctions, feelings of unity with divine reality or cosmos, sense of accessing fundamental truth, and lasting transformation of perspective. Neurologically, these states typically show maximal decrease in DMN activity coupled with unusual connectivity patterns between brain regions that normally operate separately. Phenomenologically, they represent attention so completely engaged with its object that the perceiver-perceived distinction collapses—what mystics describe as union, non-duality, or pure consciousness. While interpretations vary dramatically (union with God, realization of emptiness, experience of pure awareness), the attentional dimension appears similar: ordinary scattered attention yields to extraordinary unified attention that perceives reality without the filter of self-concern or conceptual categorization.

7. Pathologies of attention reveal what happens when this spectrum becomes distorted, providing insight into why balanced attention development matters for healthy meaning-making. Attention deficit patterns involve inability to sustain focus, constantly shifting between stimuli without depth—what might be called "salience promiscuity" where everything seems equally (and therefore not truly) significant. Attention hyper-focus pathologies involve obsessive fixation on specific concerns (health, security, purity) that drowns out other dimensions of reality—what might be called "salience monopoly" where one concern dominates perception excessively. Dissociation involves attention withdrawing from external reality entirely into internal states, severing connection with present experience. These pathologies can occur naturally or be cultivated inadvertently through certain practices: extreme asceticism might produce hyper-focus on bodily discomfort, certain meditation approaches might trigger dissociation, modern digital environments might promote attention deficit. Understanding these pathologies helps identify what healthy attention development requires: balance between focus and openness, capacity to shift attention appropriately, integration rather than dissociation, and calibration so attention aligns with what genuinely matters rather than getting hijacked by trivial or distorted concerns.

8. Developmental aspects of attention capacity show how this spectrum expands across the lifespan and can be cultivated intentionally. Children typically have shorter attention spans that gradually lengthen with maturation and education. Adolescents often experience attention fluctuations due to neurological reorganization. Adults can continue developing attention capacity through practice, though plasticity may decrease with age without intentional maintenance. Aging may bring both challenges (cognitive slowing, sensory decline) and opportunities (wisdom, perspective, decreased distraction by trivial concerns). Different life stages thus present different attentional starting points and possibilities for cultivation. Meaning systems that address the full lifespan typically offer age-appropriate attention training: simple repetitive practices for children, identity-forming rituals for adolescents, householder disciplines for adults, contemplative practices for elders. This developmental approach recognizes that attention capacity isn't fixed but evolves, and that profound salience perception requires building on existing attentional foundations rather than imposing one-size-fits-all practices. It also suggests why some people struggle with certain practices—they may not have developed the foundational attentional capacities those practices require.

9. Cultural technologies for attention management represent practical applications of understanding the attention spectrum across human history. Pre-modern cultures built attention supports into daily life through seasonal rhythms, ritual calendars, sacred architecture that directed attention upward, and craft traditions requiring sustained focus. Modernity introduced new attention challenges: industrialization created fragmented attention through repetitive tasks, urbanization created sensory overload, media created constant interruption. Contemporary digital culture creates unprecedented attention fragmentation through notifications, multitasking expectations, and endless content streams. Responses include both revival of traditional attention practices (meditation, contemplative prayer) and development of new technologies (attention training apps, digital minimalism, focus-enhancing environments). These responses recognize that attention is not merely individual capacity but is shaped by environmental structures, and that creating conditions for healthy attention requires intentional design at both individual and societal levels. They also suggest that contemporary meaning systems need to address attention management explicitly, as traditional environmental supports have eroded while new distractions have multiplied.

10. As we examine the attention spectrum's relationship to profound salience, several key insights emerge for understanding the perceptual function of meaning systems. First, attention is not neutral window on reality but active process of selection and enhancement—what we attend to shapes what we can perceive as significant. Second, attention capacity exists along a continuum that can be developed through practice—profound salience isn't random gift but cultivable skill. Third, different traditions develop attention differently based on their understanding of what deserves attention—some cultivating focused concentration, others open awareness, others absorbed engagement. Fourth, attention pathologies reveal risks when development becomes imbalanced—healthy meaning-making requires attention that can focus deeply but also shift appropriately. Fifth, contemporary challenges require conscious attention management as traditional environmental supports have diminished. These insights help explain why attention training appears universally in meaning systems—it's developing the fundamental perceptual capacity needed to experience whatever a tradition considers ultimately real as actually real and compelling. The attention spectrum thus represents the tuning mechanism through which profound salience becomes accessible, with different traditions tuning consciousness to different frequencies of significance.

### 4.4: Contemplative Traditions as Salience Training Systems

1. Contemplative traditions across cultures represent sophisticated systems for training attention to perceive what each tradition considers ultimately real or significant, effectively functioning as salience education programs that reorient perception toward particular understandings of profound reality. These systems typically include both theoretical frameworks explaining why certain realities deserve attention and practical methods for cultivating sensitivity to those realities. Buddhist meditation trains perception to notice impermanence, interdependence, and the constructed nature of self. Christian contemplation trains perception to recognize divine presence in all things. Hindu yoga trains perception to recognize the Self (Atman) underlying changing phenomena. Indigenous practices train perception to recognize spiritual presence in natural world. Despite different metaphysical frameworks, these traditions share structural similarities: they identify certain dimensions of reality as ultimately significant, diagnose ordinary perception as missing or distorting these dimensions, prescribe practices to correct perceptual habits, and describe progressive stages of perceptual refinement. Understanding contemplative traditions as salience training systems helps explain their centrality in meaning systems—they're not optional additions but core perceptual education without which the tradition's ultimate concerns remain abstract concepts rather than lived realities.

2. Buddhist meditation systems offer particularly well-developed examples of salience training with clear progression from basic to advanced perceptual skills. The foundational practice of mindfulness of breathing (ānāpānasati) begins training attention to notice subtle sensations often overlooked in ordinary consciousness. Mindfulness of body (kāyagatāsati) expands this to include bodily sensations, cultivating sensitivity to physical reality as it actually is. Mindfulness of feelings (vedanānupassanā) trains attention to distinguish pleasant, unpleasant, and neutral tones in experience, revealing how we automatically grasp or avoid based on these tones. Mindfulness of mind (cittānupassanā) trains attention to notice mental states as they arise and pass, revealing their impermanent nature. Mindfulness of phenomena (dhammānupassanā) trains attention to perceive reality through frameworks like the five aggregates or dependent origination. This progressive training systematically deconstructs ordinary perception that takes things as solid, separate, and substantial, reconstructing perception to experience reality as transient, interdependent, and empty of inherent existence. The salience being cultivated is sensitivity to these characteristics as ultimately real—what appears ordinary becomes profoundly significant when seen as impermanent, what appears separate becomes profoundly connected when seen as interdependent, what appears substantial becomes profoundly empty when seen as dependently originated.

3. Christian contemplative traditions train salience toward different ultimate realities but with similar systematic approaches to perceptual reeducation. The Ignatian Spiritual Exercises guide practitioners through weeks of structured prayer, imagination, and reflection designed to help them "find God in all things"—training attention to perceive divine presence in creation, scripture, daily events, and interior movements. Carmelite contemplation emphasizes silent prayer that moves beyond concepts and images to direct experience of God's presence. Benedictine lectio divina (sacred reading) trains attention to listen for God's voice through scripture, moving from reading to meditation to prayer to contemplation. Eastern Orthodox hesychasm uses the Jesus Prayer and attention to breath to cultivate constant remembrance of God. Despite different methods, these traditions share goal of training perception to recognize what they consider ultimately real—God's presence, will, and action in the world—as actually perceptible rather than merely believed. The salience being cultivated is sensitivity to divine reality underlying and permeating ordinary reality, transforming how practitioners perceive everything from nature to human relationships to suffering.

4. Hindu yoga systems offer another approach to salience training with emphasis on unifying perception through stages of practice. The eight limbs of Patanjali's yoga progress from ethical preparation (yamas and niyamas) through physical postures (āsana) and breath control (prānāyāma) to sensory withdrawal (pratyāhāra), concentration (dhāranā), meditation (dhyāna), and absorption (samādhi). This progression systematically trains attention: first stabilizing it in the body, then refining it through breath awareness, then internalizing it by withdrawing from external distractions, then focusing it single-pointedly, then sustaining that focus, then merging with the object of focus. The salience being cultivated progresses from gross to subtle: first noticing bodily alignment and breath patterns, then noticing subtle energy flows (prāna), then noticing mental patterns, then perceiving the witnessing consciousness itself (purusa) distinct from changing phenomena (prakrti). Advanced practitioners report perceiving reality as unified consciousness manifesting as diversity—what appears as separate beings and objects reveals itself as expressions of one underlying reality. This represents a different salience education than Buddhist emptiness or Christian divine presence but shares structural similarity: systematic retraining of perception to recognize what the tradition considers ultimately real.

5. Sufi practices within Islam train salience toward perception of divine unity (tawhid) and presence through methods combining prayer, chanting, movement, and poetry. Dhikr (remembrance of God) involves repetitive invocation of divine names, training attention to return constantly to awareness of God. Sama (spiritual listening) involves music and poetry that evoke longing for divine, training emotional and aesthetic sensitivity to divine beauty. Muraqaba (watchfulness) involves meditation on divine attributes, training perception to recognize those attributes manifesting in creation. The whirling dance of Mevlevi dervishes uses spinning to induce altered states where ordinary perception yields to experience of divine unity. These practices aim to transform perception so that, as Sufi poet Rumi expressed, "The minute I heard my first love story, I started looking for you, not knowing how blind that was. Lovers don't finally meet somewhere. They're in each other all along." The salience being cultivated is sensitivity to divine reality as both transcendent and immanent, both beyond creation and present within it, with practices designed to break habitual perception that sees only separation.

6. Indigenous contemplative practices often embedded in daily life rather than separate sessions train salience toward spiritual dimensions of natural world and community. Vision quests involve solitary time in nature fasting and praying until spiritual guidance emerges through dreams, visions, or animal encounters—training attention to perceive spiritual significance in natural phenomena. Sweat lodge ceremonies use heat, darkness, and prayer to induce states where participants perceive connections between human, natural, and spiritual realms. Dance and drumming ceremonies synchronize participants' attention and energy, creating collective perception of spiritual presence. Storytelling transmits perceptual frameworks that train listeners to see moral and spiritual significance in events. These practices typically don't separate "contemplation" from other activities but integrate perceptual training into hunting, gathering, crafting, healing, and community life. The salience being cultivated is sensitivity to the aliveness and spiritual presence of all things—what appears as inert object or resource reveals itself as relative with whom one has reciprocal responsibilities. This represents ecological and relational salience education that perceives reality as fundamentally interconnected community rather than collection of separate entities.

7. Common elements across contemplative traditions reveal universal principles of salience training despite different contents. Most include preliminary ethical preparation recognizing that certain behaviors and attitudes cloud perception while others clarify it. Most involve basic attention training to stabilize and focus mind, recognizing that scattered attention cannot perceive subtle realities. Most progress from gross to subtle objects of attention, building perceptual capacity gradually. Most include guidance from teachers who have themselves traversed the path, providing maps and correcting misunderstandings. Most describe stages of development with increasing depth and stability of perception. Most recognize that intellectual understanding alone is insufficient—direct perceptual experience is essential. Most acknowledge that progress requires sustained practice over time, not occasional effort. Most balance effort and surrender—intentional practice creates conditions for perception to open spontaneously. These common elements suggest that salience training follows natural principles of perceptual learning, with different traditions applying these principles to different ultimate concerns. They represent cultural refinements of universal human capacity to educate attention toward what each culture deems most real and significant.

8. Measuring progress in salience perception presents challenges but various traditions have developed criteria that map roughly onto contemporary psychological measures. Buddhist texts describe stages of concentration (jhanas) with specific phenomenological markers: decreased discursive thinking, increased pleasure and equanimity, refined attention, and eventually cessation of ordinary perception. Christian mystics describe degrees of prayer from vocal to mental to affective to contemplative, with corresponding changes in how God is perceived. Hindu yoga describes samadhi states with increasing absorption and unification. Contemporary research uses psychological scales to measure similar dimensions: depth of absorption, clarity of perception, stability of attention, integration of experience, and transformation of perspective. While traditional and modern measures use different language, they point toward similar developments: increased capacity to sustain attention on subtle objects, decreased distraction by trivial concerns, heightened sensitivity to dimensions previously overlooked, and reorganization of perceptual priorities so that what's deemed ultimately significant actually registers as such in lived experience. These measures help validate that contemplative practices produce genuine perceptual changes, not just subjective beliefs.

9. Transmission of contemplative methods across generations and cultures reveals both conservation of core practices and adaptation to new contexts. Traditional lineages maintain practices through direct teacher-student relationships, preserving methods while allowing for individual adaptation. Texts preserve descriptions of practices and experiences, though these often require living interpretation. In contemporary globalized context, contemplative practices increasingly cross traditional boundaries: Buddhist mindfulness adapted for secular contexts, Christian centering prayer influenced by Eastern meditation, yoga divorced from Hindu metaphysics for health benefits. These cross-cultural transmissions test which elements are essential to salience training and which are cultural packaging. Research suggests that basic attention training elements transfer across contexts effectively, while specific metaphysical interpretations and cultural frameworks may or may not be necessary depending on goals. This adaptability suggests that salience training has both universal components (attention stabilization, perceptual refinement) and particular components (what specific reality is being perceived as salient). It also raises questions about whether practices divorced from their traditional frameworks can produce the same depth of salience perception, or whether the framework itself shapes the perception in crucial ways.

10. As we examine contemplative traditions as salience training systems, several implications emerge for understanding the perceptual function of meaning systems. First, profound salience is not merely spontaneous occurrence but cultivable capacity—traditions have developed sophisticated educational methods for this cultivation. Second, different traditions cultivate sensitivity to different ultimate realities, explaining why practitioners from different traditions have different profound experiences—they're training perception differently. Third, these training systems typically follow universal principles of perceptual learning despite different contents, suggesting natural laws governing how attention can be educated toward significance. Fourth, contemplative practices serve essential function in meaning systems by making abstract ultimate concerns become lived perceptual realities—without them, doctrines remain conceptual rather than experiential. Fifth, contemporary secular adaptations demonstrate that basic salience training can be separated from specific metaphysical frameworks, though perhaps with different outcomes. These insights help explain why contemplative practices appear so universally in meaning systems—they're the perceptual education component that trains people to actually experience what the system claims is ultimately real. They represent cultural technologies for transforming consciousness to perceive significance where ordinary perception misses it, fulfilling the perceptual function that makes meaning systems feel real rather than merely believed.

### 4.5: Scientific Awe as Secular Salience

1. Scientific engagement with the natural world frequently produces experiences of awe and wonder that function as secular forms of profound salience, providing meaning and significance without traditional religious frameworks. When a scientist contemplates the vastness of cosmic evolution, the complexity of biological systems, the elegance of mathematical equations, or the intricate patterns of physical laws, they often report experiences remarkably similar phenomenologically to religious awe: sense of vastness that transcends ordinary scales, feeling of connection to something larger than oneself, perceived encounter with profound truth, emotional responses ranging from exhilaration to humility, and lasting impact on values and perspective. These experiences demonstrate that profound salience is not exclusively tied to supernatural beliefs but emerges naturally when human attention engages deeply with reality's most fundamental patterns and scales. Scientific awe represents the perceptual function operating within naturalistic frameworks, training attention toward what science reveals as ultimately significant—cosmic order, evolutionary creativity, mathematical beauty, emergent complexity—and experiencing these as sources of meaning comparable to religious experiences of divine presence or ultimate reality.

2. The experience of cosmic perspective represents one powerful form of scientific awe that has been documented among astronomers, physicists, and even laypeople encountering images from space telescopes. Viewing Earth from space or contemplating the scale of the universe—billions of galaxies each containing billions of stars, cosmic evolution over 13.8 billion years, the fine-tuning of physical constants that allow complexity to emerge—often triggers what philosopher Frank White called the "overview effect": a cognitive shift in awareness where national boundaries and human conflicts appear trivial against cosmic backdrop, while life and consciousness appear miraculously precious. Astronauts frequently report such experiences as transformative, with lasting changes in how they perceive Earth, humanity, and their own priorities. Cosmologists describe similar awe when their equations reveal unexpected connections or when observations confirm theoretical predictions about the universe's origins and structure. This cosmic awe functions as secular salience by making cosmological reality itself—rather than a creator behind it—the object of profound significance. The vastness that might provoke existential dread in one framework becomes source of meaning in another: not evidence of divine power but revelation of nature's grandeur, not proof of design but manifestation of emergent order, not call to worship but invitation to understand and protect.

3. Deep time awareness cultivated through geology, paleontology, and evolutionary biology represents another form of scientific salience that transforms perception of reality's temporal dimensions. Contemplating Earth's 4.5-billion-year history, life's 3.7-billion-year evolution, humanity's recent emergence, and the future possibilities stretching millions of years ahead creates temporal awe analogous to spatial cosmic awe. This deep time perspective reveals what biologist Stephen Jay Gould called "time's arrow and time's cycle"—both linear directionality (increasing complexity, adaptation, change) and repeating patterns (extinction events, geological cycles, evolutionary convergences). Experiencing deep time salience can produce what geologist Marcia Bjornerud calls "timefulness"—a mindful awareness of Earth's long history and humanity's place within it. This awareness functions meaningfully by embedding human concerns within vastly larger temporal frames: our civilizations become brief experiments in a long planetary story, our environmental impacts become measurable against geological timescales, our existence becomes gift of cumulative evolutionary creativity. Like cosmic awe, temporal awe doesn't require supernatural beliefs but finds significance in natural processes themselves—evolution's patient experimentation, geology's slow transformations, extinction's creative destruction.

4. Complexity appreciation developed through biology, ecology, and systems science represents a third form of scientific salience focused on nature's intricate patterns rather than vast scales. Studying the molecular machinery inside cells, the neural networks in brains, the symbiotic relationships in ecosystems, or the emergent properties of complex systems often produces what biologist E.O. Wilson called "biophilia"—the innate human attraction to life and lifelike processes. This appreciation goes beyond intellectual understanding to aesthetic and emotional responses: wonder at DNA's information storage, awe at photosynthesis's energy transformation, marvel at consciousness emergence from neural networks, humility before ecosystems' delicate balances. Complexity salience finds significance in nature's creativity—the ways simple components self-organize into complex wholes, the evolutionary innovations that solve problems, the adaptive intelligence distributed throughout living systems. This represents a different form of secular meaning-making than cosmic or temporal awe: not significance through vastness but through intricacy, not meaning from being small in large universe but from participating in creative processes that generate complexity from simplicity, order from chaos, consciousness from matter.

5. Mathematical beauty experienced by mathematicians and theoretical physicists represents a particularly abstract form of scientific salience where significance emerges from formal patterns rather than empirical observations. Mathematicians frequently describe certain proofs, theorems, or equations as "beautiful," "elegant," or "profound"—experiences that philosopher Bertrand Russell described as "sublime" in their combination of truth and beauty. These experiences share features with aesthetic and religious experiences: sense of encountering timeless truth, emotional response to elegance and harmony, perception of deep structure underlying surface complexity. Nobel physicist Paul Dirac declared, "It is more important to have beauty in one's equations than to have them fit experiment," expressing the profound salience mathematical beauty holds for theoretical scientists. This beauty isn't merely subjective preference but often correlates with truth—what mathematicians find beautiful frequently proves more fundamental and generative. Mathematical salience thus provides meaning through appreciation of abstract reality's elegant structure—what physicist Eugene Wigner called "the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences." The meaning lies not in practical applications but in the human mind's capacity to perceive and participate in mathematical reality that appears to exist independently of human invention.

6. Scientific epiphanies or "eureka moments" represent intense forms of scientific salience where understanding breaks through suddenly, often after prolonged struggle. Archimedes' bath-inspired insight about displacement, Kekulé's dream-inspired understanding of benzene's ring structure, Darwin's gradual realization of natural selection's implications—these moments share phenomenological features with mystical experiences: sudden clarity after confusion, sense of encountering truth directly, emotional intensity, and lasting transformation of understanding. Contemporary scientists report similar experiences: the moment a complex dataset reveals a pattern, conflicting evidence suddenly coheres into new theory, mathematical derivation yields unexpectedly elegant result. Neuroscience research shows these insight moments involve specific brain patterns: sudden gamma-wave bursts in right temporal lobe, preceded by alpha-wave increases suggesting suppression of habitual thinking. These epiphanies function as scientific salience by making abstract understanding feel personally significant and reality-revealing. They provide meaning not through doctrine but through direct apprehension of nature's workings—what philosopher Michael Polanyi called "personal knowledge" that combines objective truth with subjective commitment. The scientist doesn't just know something; they feel its truth and significance, often motivating years of dedicated work to develop and defend the insight.

7. How science cultivates wonder represents an important dimension of scientific salience that addresses criticisms of science as disenchanting or reductionist. While some scientific explanations might seem to "explain away" mystery by reducing phenomena to mechanisms, many scientists report the opposite: deeper understanding increases wonder by revealing greater complexity, more elegant patterns, or more profound questions. Biologist Richard Dawkins, despite his atheism, writes passionately about science's capacity to inspire "appreciative wonder" at reality's grandeur. Physicist Brian Greene describes how understanding general relativity or quantum mechanics doesn't diminish but enhances cosmic awe. This suggests that scientific salience operates through what philosopher Philip Kitcher calls "understanding beyond reduction"—grasping how phenomena fit into larger patterns without reducing them to nothing but their components. Science cultivates wonder through specific practices: detailed observation revealing unnoticed beauty, experimentation revealing hidden capacities, modeling revealing elegant mathematics, theorizing revealing unified explanations. These practices train attention toward what science deems significant—patterns, mechanisms, relationships, principles—and develop capacity to find meaning in understanding itself rather than in supernatural explanations. Scientific wonder thus represents not absence of meaning but different kind of meaning-making that finds significance in comprehensibility rather than mystery, in natural processes rather than supernatural intervention.

8. Secular rituals of awe represent institutionalized practices that cultivate scientific salience in public contexts, functioning similarly to religious rituals but with naturalistic content. Planetarium shows use immersive technology to induce cosmic perspective. Natural history museums display evolutionary deep time through fossils and reconstructions. Science centers create interactive experiences of physical principles. Public lectures by scientists share wonder of discoveries. Nature documentaries reveal ecological complexity. These rituals serve functions parallel to religious rituals: they create shared experiences of awe, transmit cultural values about what deserves attention and appreciation, reinforce community around shared meaning, and provide opportunities for perspective transformation. They differ in content but not structure: where religious rituals point toward divine reality, secular scientific rituals point toward natural reality; where religious rituals use sacred symbols, scientific rituals use scientific representations; where religious rituals invoke supernatural presence, scientific rituals invoke natural wonder. This structural similarity suggests that the ritual function in meaning systems can be fulfilled with different contents, and that scientific institutions have developed practices that serve similar psychological and social needs as religious institutions, just with naturalistic rather than supernatural frameworks.

9. Scientific salience differs from religious salience in several important ways that reveal different approaches to meaning-making while sharing underlying perceptual dynamics. Scientific salience typically emphasizes comprehensibility—finding significance in what can be understood through evidence and reason. Religious salience often emphasizes mystery—finding significance in what transcends ordinary understanding. Scientific salience usually focuses on natural processes as they are. Religious salience often focuses on supernatural realities behind or beyond natural processes. Scientific salience generally cultivates wonder through understanding mechanisms. Religious salience often cultivates awe through encounter with transcendent presence. Scientific salience tends to be provisional—open to revision with new evidence. Religious salience tends to be dogmatic—resistant to contradiction by evidence. Despite these differences, both engage similar perceptual capacities: attention to what feels ultimately significant, emotional responses of awe and wonder, sense of connection to larger reality, and motivation to align life with what feels most real and important. This suggests that the perceptual function operates across different metaphysical frameworks, with each framework cultivating sensitivity to different objects but through similar psychological processes.

10. As we examine scientific awe as secular salience, several implications emerge for understanding meaning systems in contemporary pluralistic societies. First, profound salience is not inherently supernatural—naturalistic frameworks can and do generate experiences of ultimate significance. Second, science doesn't necessarily eliminate meaning but can provide alternative sources through understanding nature's grandeur, complexity, and elegance. Third, scientific institutions have developed practices and rituals that cultivate awe and wonder parallel to religious practices. Fourth, individuals can experience both scientific and religious salience, sometimes separately, sometimes integrated. Fifth, scientific salience typically emphasizes different values than religious salience—understanding over mystery, evidence over revelation, provisionality over certainty—but can fulfill similar psychological needs for meaning, perspective, and connection to larger reality. These insights help explain why many scientists maintain deep sense of meaning and wonder despite rejecting supernatural beliefs, and why science can function as meaning system for some people. They also suggest possibilities for dialogue between scientific and religious approaches to meaning: rather than conflict over truth claims, perhaps recognition that both cultivate profound salience, just toward different objects with different methods. The perceptual function thus operates across the science-religion spectrum, with human capacity for awe and wonder being directed toward different understandings of what ultimately matters in reality.

### 4.6: Aesthetic Experiences of Profound Significance

1. Aesthetic experiences—encounters with beauty, sublimity, and artistic expression—frequently serve as portals to profound salience, providing access to dimensions of meaning that feel ultimately significant without necessarily invoking religious or scientific frameworks. When people encounter breathtaking natural scenery, profoundly moving musical compositions, deeply resonant visual art, or powerfully affecting literature, they often report experiences that share phenomenological features with religious awe or scientific wonder: sense of encountering something that matters fundamentally, emotional intensity ranging from joy to melancholy to transcendence, feeling of connection to larger reality or deeper truth, and lasting impact on values and perspective. These aesthetic experiences demonstrate that profound salience can emerge through sensory and emotional channels as well as through conceptual or spiritual ones, and that artistic creation and appreciation represent important meaning-making practices across cultures. Understanding aesthetic salience helps explain why art has so often served religious purposes historically while also functioning as autonomous source of meaning in secular contexts—it trains attention toward qualities and patterns that feel significant in themselves, whether interpreted as divine revelation, human expression, or natural beauty.

2. Visual art functions as salience training by directing attention to aspects of reality that might otherwise go unnoticed, framing them in ways that reveal deeper significance. A landscape painting might train viewers to notice particular qualities of light, color, and composition in nature. A portrait might train attention to subtle expressions conveying complex human emotions. Abstract art might train perception of formal relationships—balance, tension, rhythm—that operate beneath representational content. Religious art explicitly trains salience toward spiritual realities: icons in Eastern Orthodox tradition train perception of divine presence through particular visual conventions; Renaissance altarpieces train perception of sacred stories through narrative composition; Buddhist mandalas train perception of cosmic order through geometric patterns. Even secular art trains salience toward what artists deem significant: social conditions needing attention, psychological states worth exploring, formal relationships revealing underlying order. The artist's selection and framing says, in effect, "Pay attention to this—this matters." Viewers who engage deeply learn to see reality through the artist's perceptual lens, potentially incorporating that lens into their own way of noticing what's significant. This educational function explains why art appreciation is often described as expanding perception rather than merely providing pleasure.

3. Music represents perhaps the most direct aesthetic pathway to profound salience due to its capacity to bypass conceptual processing and evoke emotional and physiological responses that feel deeply significant. Certain musical compositions—whether religious hymns, classical symphonies, folk melodies, or contemporary pieces—consistently evoke experiences described as transcendent, numinous, or spiritually elevating across diverse listeners. Research suggests several reasons for music's power: rhythmic entrainment synchronizes listeners' physiological states; harmonic progressions create tension and resolution patterns that mirror emotional experiences; melodic contours evoke movement and yearning; timbral qualities trigger memories and associations; structural complexity rewards attentive listening. Religious traditions have long harnessed this power: Gregorian chant induces meditative states through monophonic simplicity; Sufi qawwali uses rhythm and repetition to induce ecstasy; gospel music combines rhythm, harmony, and lyrics to evoke spiritual presence. Secular music similarly induces profound experiences: Beethoven's symphonies evoke struggle and triumph; blues expresses suffering and resilience; ambient music creates spacious awareness. In all cases, music trains salience toward emotional and existential dimensions that language alone cannot capture, providing meaning through direct feeling rather than conceptual understanding.

4. Literary art cultivates salience through narrative and language that frame human experience in ways that reveal deeper patterns and significances. A novel might train readers to notice psychological complexities in themselves and others. Poetry might train attention to linguistic nuances and metaphorical connections. Drama might train perception of social dynamics and moral dilemmas. Religious scriptures use literary forms to train salience toward divine realities: parables train perception of spiritual truths in everyday situations; psalms train emotional expression toward God; prophetic poetry trains perception of social injustice as violation of divine will. Secular literature similarly trains salience: dystopian fiction trains attention to political dangers, coming-of-age stories train perception of identity formation, nature writing trains attention to ecological relationships. The act of reading deeply requires surrendering to an author's perceptual world, temporarily seeing reality through their framing of what matters. This temporary surrender can become lasting education if the framing resonates and integrates into the reader's own perception. Literary salience thus operates through imagination—training capacity to perceive significance in possibilities, not just actualities—which explains why fiction can feel as meaningful as nonfiction, and why imagined worlds can illuminate real-world significance.

5. Performance arts like theater, dance, and film cultivate salience through embodied participation—either as performer or audience—in representations of human experience that feel ultimately significant. Live theater creates shared presence where fictional events feel real, training attention to interpersonal dynamics, moral choices, and existential dilemmas. Dance uses movement to express what language cannot, training perception of embodied emotion, relational patterns, and kinetic beauty. Film combines multiple sensory channels to create immersive experiences that can trigger profound emotional and cognitive responses. Religious performance arts train salience toward spiritual realities: passion plays reenact sacred stories to make them feel present; ritual dance embodies spiritual forces; religious film evokes devotion. Secular performance similarly trains salience toward human realities: political theater trains attention to injustice, contemporary dance trains perception of modern alienation, documentary film trains attention to social issues. Performance's power lies in its "as if" quality—temporary suspension of ordinary reality to explore other possibilities—which can reveal aspects of ordinary reality previously unnoticed. By participating in these "as if" worlds, whether as creator or spectator, people educate their perception of what matters in the "as is" world.

6. Architectural and environmental aesthetics cultivate salience through spatial experiences that frame human existence within particular understandings of significance. Sacred architecture trains perception of divine presence through space: Gothic cathedrals use verticality to point toward heaven, light to symbolize divine illumination, and proportion to reflect cosmic harmony; Hindu temples use mandala patterns to map cosmic order; Islamic mosques use geometric patterns to express infinite divine unity. Secular architecture similarly trains salience toward human values: democratic buildings emphasize transparency and accessibility, museums create spaces for contemplative engagement with art, sustainable design emphasizes connection to natural systems. Natural environments themselves train salience when experienced aesthetically: mountains evoke sublimity, forests evoke mystery, oceans evoke vastness, gardens evoke harmony. These spatial experiences educate perception by embodying values and worldviews in physical form, creating environments that continually reinforce particular understandings of what matters through how space is organized, experienced, and moved through. Unlike portable art, architectural salience operates through immersion—we inhabit these spaces, and they subtly shape our perception through repeated exposure.

7. Cross-cultural aesthetic differences reveal how salience training varies while maintaining underlying structural similarities. Different cultures develop distinct aesthetic standards: Japanese aesthetics might emphasize wabi-sabi (imperfect, impermanent, incomplete), Indian aesthetics might emphasize rasa (flavors or essences), Western aesthetics might emphasize formal harmony, Indigenous aesthetics might emphasize relationship to place and community. These differences reflect different understandings of what's ultimately significant: transience versus essence, formal beauty versus relational harmony. Yet despite these differences, all cultures develop aesthetic traditions that train attention toward what they value, and all report profound aesthetic experiences that feel meaningful. This suggests that aesthetic salience represents universal human capacity that gets culturally shaped toward particular values and perceptions. It also suggests why aesthetic education matters for cross-cultural understanding: learning to appreciate another culture's aesthetics means learning to perceive reality through their salience framework, at least partially. This perceptual bridge can create deeper understanding than intellectual exchange alone, as it operates at the level of how reality is experienced rather than just how it's conceptualized.

8. The relationship between aesthetic experience and meaning-making operates through what philosopher John Dewey called "having an experience"—moments when life feels particularly integrated, significant, and memorable. Aesthetic experiences often serve as paradigmatic cases of such integrated moments: attention fully engaged, perception heightened, emotion congruent with object, sense of wholeness and completion. These integrated experiences become reference points for meaning—what psychologist Dan McAdams calls "nuclear episodes" in life stories that organize understanding of who we are and what matters. Religious aesthetics explicitly creates such integrated experiences around spiritual themes: a beautiful liturgy, moving hymn, or stunning stained glass window can become touchstone for faith. Secular aesthetics creates integrated experiences around human themes: a powerful novel, affecting song, or breathtaking landscape can become touchstone for personal values. In both cases, aesthetic salience provides raw material for meaning construction: experiences that feel deeply significant become building blocks for larger meaning systems, whether those systems are explicitly religious, philosophical, or personal. This explains why people often point to aesthetic experiences when explaining what matters to them—these experiences provide perceptual evidence of significance that abstract beliefs alone cannot supply.

9. Contemporary challenges to aesthetic salience come from several directions in modern societies that may undermine this important meaning-making pathway. Commercialization often reduces art to entertainment or commodity, divorcing it from deeper significance. Digital reproduction floods us with images and sounds without context, potentially numbing aesthetic sensitivity. Specialization separates art from everyday life, making it seem like separate domain rather than integrated dimension of experience. These challenges have inspired various responses: the slow art movement encourages prolonged engagement with single works; mindfulness practices train attention to aesthetic qualities in ordinary moments; community art projects reconnect creation with shared meaning; aesthetic education initiatives in schools cultivate sensitivity from early age. These responses recognize that aesthetic salience requires cultivation—it's not automatic but needs education and supportive environments. They also suggest that contemporary meaning systems need to address aesthetic dimensions explicitly, as traditional integration of art with daily life and ritual has eroded in many contexts. Without intentional cultivation, this important pathway to profound salience may become less accessible, potentially impoverishing meaning-making capacities.

10. As we examine aesthetic experiences of profound significance, several implications emerge for understanding the perceptual function in meaning systems. First, aesthetic salience represents an important and potentially universal pathway to experiencing significance that complements religious and scientific pathways. Second, different art forms train attention toward different dimensions of significance—visual art toward visual patterns, music toward emotional patterns, literature toward narrative patterns, etc.—suggesting comprehensive aesthetic education develops multidimensional salience sensitivity. Third, aesthetic experiences often serve as foundational "raw material" for meaning construction—moments that feel significant before they're interpreted within larger frameworks. Fourth, cultural differences in aesthetics represent different salience training programs—learning another culture's aesthetics means learning to notice what they notice as significant. Fifth, contemporary challenges require intentional cultivation of aesthetic sensitivity as traditional supports have diminished. These insights help explain why art has been so central to meaning systems across history—it's not merely decoration but essential perceptual education that trains people to experience certain dimensions of reality as significant. Even in secular contexts, aesthetic experience continues to provide access to profound salience, suggesting this pathway remains vital for human meaning-making regardless of metaphysical commitments. The aesthetic dimension thus represents a crucial component of the perceptual function, training attention toward qualities, patterns, and experiences that feel ultimately significant through sensory, emotional, and imaginative channels.

### 4.7: Moral Intuitions as Salience Markers

1. Moral experiences—feelings that certain actions are right or wrong, just or unjust, compassionate or cruel—often carry the phenomenological weight of profound salience, registering not merely as preferences or conventions but as perceptions of objective significance about how we should treat one another and ourselves. When people witness injustice, they often describe not just intellectual disagreement but visceral reaction—something feels fundamentally wrong. When people perform acts of compassion, they often describe not just satisfaction but sense of alignment with how things should be. These moral intuitions function as salience markers, directing attention toward what feels ethically significant and demanding response. Different ethical traditions frame these intuitions differently: as divine commands, as natural law, as rational principles, as emotional responses, as social contracts. But across frameworks, moral experience often shares features with other profound salience experiences: sense of encountering objective reality (moral truth rather than mere opinion), emotional intensity (outrage, guilt, elevation), feeling of connection to larger order (cosmic justice, human dignity, natural harmony), and motivational force (compelling action rather than just contemplation). Understanding moral intuition as salience helps explain why ethical concerns feel so deeply meaningful—they're not just calculations but perceptions of significance that shape how we experience reality itself.

2. The experience of moral certainty in particular situations represents a clear case of moral salience where rightness or wrongness feels perceptually evident rather than deductively concluded. A person witnessing cruelty to a child doesn't typically reason, "If we assume the principle of minimizing suffering, and if children's suffering is especially bad, then this action is wrong"—they simply perceive the wrongness directly. A person risking their life to save another doesn't typically calculate utility—they respond to the perceived value of the other's life. These immediate moral perceptions share features with aesthetic "seeing as"—we don't see mere physical movements but see them as cruel or heroic. Philosophers debate whether these perceptions represent genuine access to moral reality or psychological projections, but phenomenologically they function as salience: they make certain aspects of situations stand out as demanding attention and response. Different ethical traditions educate this moral perception differently: virtue ethics trains perception to recognize what the virtuous person would see, deontology trains perception of duties and rights, utilitarianism trains perception of consequences, care ethics trains perception of relationships. But all aim to cultivate reliable moral salience—capacity to notice what matters ethically in complex situations.

3. Conscience experiences represent internalized moral salience that operates even without external observers, suggesting that moral significance can register subjectively as well as intersubjectively. Feelings of guilt after wrongdoing, feelings of integrity after right action, internal dialogue about ethical dilemmas—these conscience phenomena function as salience markers directing attention to moral dimensions of our own actions and character. Religious traditions often frame conscience as divine voice or natural law written on the heart. Secular traditions frame it as internalized social norms or rational self-reflection. But across frameworks, conscience shares features with other salience experiences: it interrupts ordinary consciousness with moral concern, carries emotional weight, feels authoritative (telling us what we should do rather than what we want to do), and connects individual actions to larger frameworks of value. The development of conscience from childhood through adulthood represents moral salience education—learning what deserves guilt, what warrants self-respect, how to weigh competing values. This education occurs through modeling, instruction, consequences, and reflection, gradually shaping what registers as morally significant. Mature conscience represents reliable moral perception that notices ethical dimensions others might miss and responds appropriately even without external enforcement.

4. Empathy and compassion represent other-focused moral salience where another's suffering or wellbeing becomes perceptually significant and motivationally compelling. When we see someone in pain and feel moved to help, we're experiencing their suffering as salient—as mattering in a way that demands response. Mirror neuron research suggests neurological basis for this perceptual capacity: seeing others' pain activates similar brain regions as experiencing pain ourselves, creating shared salience. Compassion extends this further by adding motivation to alleviate suffering. Different traditions cultivate this moral perception differently: Buddhist loving-kindness meditation explicitly trains attention to others' wellbeing, Christian agape emphasizes seeing Christ in others, humanist empathy emphasizes shared humanity, care ethics emphasizes attention to particular relationships. But all aim to expand the circle of whose suffering registers as salient—from self to family to community to all beings. This expansion represents moral salience education: learning to notice and care about suffering that might otherwise be overlooked or dismissed. The hardest moral challenges often involve failures of this perceptual capacity—not seeing certain others as fully human, not registering their suffering as significant, not perceiving our connection to them.

5. Justice perception represents another form of moral salience where fairness, equity, and desert register as significant features of situations. Witnessing unequal treatment often triggers immediate perception of injustice before any analysis of principles. Experiencing fair procedures feels right in ways that transcend outcome satisfaction. These justice perceptions function as salience markers that certain distributions or procedures matter fundamentally. Research suggests humans have innate fairness detectors that develop early in childhood and operate across cultures, though their specific applications vary. Different traditions frame justice differently: as divine commandment, as social contract, as natural right, as utilitarian calculation. But across frameworks, injustice typically registers phenomenologically as salient—as violating how things should be. This salience explains why injustice often provokes strong emotional responses (righteous anger) and motivated action (protest, reform). Moral education includes justice salience training: learning to notice subtle inequities, recognizing structural injustices, perceiving when merit is rewarded or ignored. Like other salience forms, justice perception can be cultivated or dulled through experience and education, explaining why some people become sensitive to injustices others overlook.

6. Cross-cultural moral foundations research by psychologists like Jonathan Haidt identifies several recurrent dimensions of moral salience that appear across diverse societies, though weighted differently. Care/harm sensitivity registers suffering and need as significant. Fairness/cheating sensitivity registers equity and reciprocity as significant. Loyalty/betrayal sensitivity registers group commitment as significant. Authority/subversion sensitivity registers hierarchy and respect as significant. Sanctity/degradation sensitivity registers purity and sacredness as significant. Liberty/oppression sensitivity registers freedom and tyranny as significant. These foundations represent different channels of moral salience—different aspects of situations that can register as morally significant. Different cultures and subcultures weight these foundations differently: liberal societies often emphasize care and fairness, conservative societies often add loyalty, authority, and sanctity, traditional societies vary in their combinations. Moral education within any culture involves training attention toward the foundations it values—teaching children what to notice as morally significant. Moral disagreement often involves different salience patterns: one person notices harm where another notices disrespect, one perceives impurity where another perceives liberty restriction. Understanding moral intuition as salience helps explain these differences not as rational disagreements but as perceptual differences—people literally notice different moral dimensions because their attention has been educated differently.

7. Moral epiphanies and conversion experiences represent intense forms of moral salience where ethical understanding transforms suddenly, often with lasting impact. A person might suddenly perceive the wrongness of behavior they previously considered acceptable, or recognize the humanity of a group they previously dehumanized, or understand their privilege in new ways. These moral breakthroughs share features with other profound salience experiences: sudden clarity, emotional intensity, sense of encountering truth, and motivational transformation. Historical examples abound: slaveholders becoming abolitionists after perceiving slaves' humanity, patriarchs becoming feminists after perceiving gender injustice, consumers becoming environmentalists after perceiving ecological connections. These transformations often begin with perceptual shifts—seeing differently rather than just thinking differently—followed by conceptual frameworks that explain and extend the new perception. Moral education that aims at transformation often includes practices designed to trigger such perceptual shifts: exposure to different perspectives, immersion in others' experiences, guided reflection that reveals unnoticed dimensions. This suggests that moral growth often requires salience education—expanding what registers as ethically significant—not just logical argument about principles.

8. Training moral perception represents a crucial but often overlooked dimension of ethical education that complements reasoning skill development. Traditional character education often involved modeling, imitation, and habituation that trained attention toward what the virtuous person notices. Apprenticeship with moral exemplars provided opportunities to learn how they perceive situations—what dimensions they attend to, what they overlook, how they weigh competing concerns. Narrative education through stories, parables, and history provided vicarious experience of moral perception in action. Contemporary approaches include: perspective-taking exercises that imagine others' experiences, mindfulness practices that increase sensitivity to subtle reactions, case study discussions that identify relevant features, service learning that exposes students to unfamiliar realities. These methods aim to cultivate what philosopher Iris Murdoch called "attention" as fundamental moral capacity—the ability to see reality clearly without self-centered distortion. This perceptual training complements reasoning training: we need both capacity to perceive what's ethically relevant and capacity to reason about how to respond. But without the former, the latter operates on impoverished input, explaining why intelligent people can reason skillfully to unethical conclusions if their perception of the situation is limited or distorted.

9. The relationship between moral salience and other forms of profound salience reveals important connections between ethical, aesthetic, and spiritual dimensions of meaning. Many people report that moral experiences sometimes feel beautiful (acts of courage, compassion, integrity) or ugly (cruelty, betrayal, corruption), suggesting aesthetic dimension to moral perception. Many report that moral commitment sometimes feels spiritual—connected to larger purpose or cosmic justice. Many traditions explicitly connect these domains: Platonic philosophy connects goodness, truth, and beauty; religious ethics connects moral action with divine will; humanist ethics connects morality with human flourishing. These connections suggest that different forms of salience may share underlying perceptual capacities that get directed toward different objects. Moral salience directed toward right action, aesthetic salience directed toward beauty, spiritual salience directed toward ultimate reality—but all involve perception of significance that feels objective, compelling, and connected to larger order. This overlap helps explain why meaning systems typically integrate these dimensions rather than treating them separately: ethical teachings are presented as beautiful, moral exemplars as spiritually inspiring, spiritual practices as morally transformative. The integration suggests that comprehensive meaning-making requires educating multiple channels of salience that reinforce each other.

10. As we examine moral intuitions as salience markers, several implications emerge for understanding the perceptual function in meaning systems. First, moral experience often operates at perceptual level—we feel rightness or wrongness before we reason about it. Second, moral perception can be educated—we can learn to notice ethical dimensions we previously missed. Third, moral disagreement often involves different salience patterns—people literally notice different moral features. Fourth, moral transformation often begins with perceptual shifts—seeing differently precedes acting differently. Fifth, moral salience connects with other salience forms—ethical, aesthetic, and spiritual significance often interweave. These insights help explain why ethical dimensions feel so meaningful—they're not just social conventions but perceptions of significance about how we should live. They also explain why moral education requires more than teaching rules—it requires cultivating perception of what matters ethically in complex situations. Different meaning systems cultivate different moral perceptions based on their understanding of ultimate reality and human purpose: religious systems perceive moral significance as divine command or cosmic order, humanist systems perceive it as human wellbeing or dignity, ecological systems perceive it as planetary health or interspecies justice. But across these frameworks, moral intuition functions as salience marker—directing attention toward what feels ethically significant and demanding response as part of comprehensive meaning-making.
